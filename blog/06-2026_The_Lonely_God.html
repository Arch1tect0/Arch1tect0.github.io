The Lonely God...(Part I)

So I have been reading the Old Testament, the New Testament, and the Quran. One can argue that it's not necessary to read religious books anymore, but I'd say it's very important. First of all, while modern life has made a lot of things easier and more accessible, we still have a foundational need for purpose. That purpose and foundation, be it Truth or God, I'd argue is the best thing we have. Second, these books have affected us culturally across generations so much that it's undeniable, even if you're against the actual content. For me, while trying to be open-minded, I was very cautious to examine the materials with as much objectivity as one could manage. Here are my thoughts:

The God of Sacrifices (Judaism):

The Torah, or the Old Testament, is a very long and difficult read, especially for a modern reader without prior knowledge. It reads more like a genealogy written by ancient people, but I'd argue that it has value even today. Among some of the best stories is the story of Joseph—it's so human and so beautifully articulated, and it shows how God works through worldly ways to reconnect Joseph and his father years later. I almost cried by the end of that arc.

Although famous, I'm not a big fan of David's and Saul's stories. They don't come across as ideal kings, given the accounts of what they become later in life. It's worth noting that Saul and David were kings, not prophets or judges (the "judges" belong to an earlier period, described in the Book of Judges, before the monarchy began). That distinction aside, the human, flawed portrayal of these kings does seem to be a key difference between how Christianity and Judaism tend to treat their central figures. Although very old, it is a very human text. There is no divine exorcism, healing, or over-the-top miracle work.

The Son, The Father & the Holy Ghost (Christianity):

The God of kindness. The Trinity. The New Testament was written years after Jesus was crucified. Keep in mind that it was recorded by eyewitnesses or followers, and I believe there is a good chance a lot of these records shifted over time, since they were communicated orally for years before being written down—and that raises a fair question for an unbiased reader. Even so, the depth of the teachings and some of the accounts tell a deeper story—something you can still find faith in after all these years. More on Christianity later in another post.

The Humble King

When Jesus of Nazareth makes his entry into Jerusalem, he arrives on a donkey, not a war horse, to show his humility, and to show what truly matters in the eyes of God—i.e., not worldly or material status. He mainly teaches his disciples in parables—stories and examples of what and how the Father judges His children.

The Lonely God

Or my lonely God. Even though a lot of the followers, and even prophets and judges, throughout these books claim to follow God, most don't seem to care about God Himself and are oftentimes very selfish (or you could say human)—but that sounds like an excuse. I hold people of faith to a very high standard.

There's more I want to talk about in Part II. To be continued...
