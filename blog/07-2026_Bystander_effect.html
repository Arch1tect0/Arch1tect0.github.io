The Bystander Effect.

All ideas have a lifetime, correct? I mean, there is a period where they actively affect the elements of the universe, i.e. our thought processes. And if they are great enough, they pass through generations, until they finally turn into dogma or the cornerstone of our culture, language, and the very essence of our being.

But for whatever reason, or as it is the design of our world, all things come to an end eventually. All the systems that we create on top of these ideas eventually collapse.

When did thinking start? Did it start with the first living beings? Surely there were other, very simplistic sources of consciousness before us, right? That'd make a lot of sense. At the dawn of time, simple instincts ruled the world of beings. It was mainly fear and food that drove our existence. But as we became more complex, we moved to irrational fears and patterns that were beyond our souls' ability to explain. Later, religions and cultures provided a better framework for our thinking. And now it has come down to science; somehow, we forgot about the other values in life. But that's for another discussion.

I said all that as the intro to talk about the collapsing systems, ideas, and empires. Again, all systems are bound to die, economies vanish, etc. But the timeline of all that happening is dependent on a multitude of factors, one of them being the "Bystander Effect".

We, as the most conscious beings on this planet, are the main drivers and carriers of these ideas and these systems. And we naturally carry the meanings, consequences, and the system itself.

You have probably noticed this, but even if we literally don't do anything, we still affect all the elements around us. And especially, if we stand up for something and fight for an ideal, we might have even more of an effect. Unfortunately, if we don't take any moral stands and just do our part in any system, whether it be school, the company, or the government, we extend the lifetime of that system.

Maybe you've noticed that when something goes wrong, the more decent, more conscious people stand up and try to fix things. Oftentimes, there is resistance, and they are silenced.

The worst of the worst are those who stand by and let bad things happen. They are the ones with more justifiable morals and a higher threshold for wrongdoing. They let fear and other survival instincts get in the way of truth. They are the last to live, and the ones that make a system corrupt, or at least help the corruption last longer.

That brings us to the story of Mansur al-Hallaj, the Sufi and mystic figure who was publicly executed because of his claims about becoming "One with God". I won't get into the details, and you'll be the judge of what truly happened, but there is no shortage in history of people who stood up for the right thing and were dismissed, disposed of, and executed in their time.

Funny how they are the ones gaining immortality (to some degree, and maybe not always).

He was said to be (possibly exaggerated, but the point still stands) crying for the bystanders chanting for his execution, feeling sorrow for them...

Now if you ask me, I'd say that people who carry the corrupt system, the ones who are silent and compliant, they are more sinful than the oppressors...
