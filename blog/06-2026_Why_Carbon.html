Is 'Carbon Atom' the Center of the Universe?

I've been wrestling with this idea for a while. Is carbon the main catalyst of life?

It seemed very crazy at first, but carbon is capable of creating these complex bonds, and that could in fact be the essence of life almost everywhere in the universe. Well, to be a little more accurate, carbon is the way it is because of the conditions on our planet, so let's say that with temperature changes, atoms start to act differently, but the idea is still the same.

I researched for a while where and how carbon ended up on our beautiful planet, and there are several theories for that. I think they are somewhat irrelevant to this conversation, though.

So it is carbon that serves as the backbone structure for life, and then our good old water as the mediator to allow life to become more complex.

Elasticity is the most important characteristic of carbon. It's able to form super long, super elastic, and very energetically efficient bonds.

Since oil has largely similar carbon chains, a lot of what we use is created from it—from plastics to tires to all kinds of byproducts.

The Murchison meteorite, found in Victoria, Australia, is one of several examples of extraterrestrial amino acids.

Alien life—the carbon chains (amino acids) that form the main building blocks of proteins—seems to follow the same pattern, and there is more than one source of what's called carbonaceous chondrite meteorites, or even space dust.

With all that said, I still think there is the possibility of other structures that don't necessarily have to be carbon-based. They could be compound-based and different in other temperature and pressure environments. But carbon is so elegant and so simple that I don't really know. Hopefully in my lifetime we'll see other types of complex structures falling onto Earth, or us getting them from outer space somehow!
